## Set of Rules for an EM Professional Summary

### 0) Scope & Output Contract

This module is only for the short professional summary used at the top of a:

* CV / resume
* LinkedIn About intro
* executive candidate profile
* portfolio profile
* personal site profile

Default output:

Generate 3 options:

1. **Strict ATS minimal**
2. **Balanced technical leadership**
3. **Impact-forward EM**

Each option must:

* be **2–3 sentences**
* reflect the **target EM job description**
* sound credible to both **recruiters and engineering leaders**
* remain fully defensible in interview
* avoid keyword stuffing
* end with **concrete proof**

---

### 1) EM Non-Negotiables

* No fabrication
* Do not invent:

  * direct reports
  * hiring ownership
  * budgets
  * delivery scope
  * architecture ownership
  * coding depth
  * tools
  * domains
  * business impact
* Every major claim must be supported by evidence from the user’s history
* If the candidate has leadership evidence but limited formal line-management evidence:

  * **frame honestly** as tech lead / team lead / player-coach / engineering lead
  * do **not** overstate into full people management
* If the candidate has formal EM evidence but limited recent coding:

  * emphasize **technical judgment, architecture, standards, incident leadership, and technical strategy**
  * do not force deep hands-on wording
* If the JD contains a required skill that is unsupported:

  * omit it, or
  * frame it as adjacent exposure only if explicitly requested

---

### 2) Positioning Principle for EM Roles

An EM summary must present the candidate across **three layers at once**:

1. **Leadership scope**
   team leadership, coaching, hiring, performance, delivery ownership, stakeholder management, org scaling

2. **Technical authority**
   architecture, system design, platform/domain depth, quality standards, modernization, reliability, incident leadership, selective hands-on credibility

3. **Business / delivery impact**
   team scale, system scale, roadmap execution, cost/performance/reliability gains, customer or revenue impact

If any one of these three layers is missing, the summary is incomplete for EM hiring.

---

### 3) Inputs

Use as many of these as the user provides:

* job description
* target title
* scope of responsibility
* org context
* people-management evidence
* team size
* multi-team or program scope
* architecture/system ownership
* delivery outcomes
* project descriptions
* current CV
* achievement bullets
* skills list
* portfolio / GitHub context
* domain history
* additional user notes

If both JD and candidate evidence exist, build the summary from the **overlap**.

---

### 4) Signal Extraction

Extract signals from the JD into **three buckets**, not one flat list.

#### A. Leadership / Management Signals

Examples:

* Engineering Manager
* people leadership
* coaching / mentoring
* hiring
* performance management
* team building
* roadmap delivery
* stakeholder management
* cross-functional collaboration
* Agile / Scrum / Kanban / SAFe
* delivery predictability
* scaling teams
* engineering excellence

#### B. Technical Authority Signals

Examples:

* architecture
* distributed systems
* microservices
* cloud platforms
* APIs
* platform engineering
* reliability
* observability
* modernization
* CI/CD
* security
* system design
* incident management
* coding standards

#### C. Business / Domain / Outcome Signals

Examples:

* fintech
* e-commerce
* marketplaces
* enterprise platforms
* B2B SaaS
* internal developer platforms
* customer scale
* platform scale
* revenue-sensitive systems
* modernization program
* efficiency improvement
* cost optimization

---

### 5) Ranking Rules

Create two signal levels:

**A-rank**

* repeated responsibilities
* must-have leadership signals
* core technical themes
* central domain/platform context

**B-rank**

* secondary tools
* nice-to-have methods
* adjacent concepts
* supporting domain terms

For an EM summary, prefer this composition:

* **1–2 leadership keywords**
* **1–2 technical keywords**
* **0–1 domain/platform keyword**

Do not let the summary become either:

* a management cliché block, or
* a stack-heavy IC paragraph

---

### 6) Proof Mapping

Every keyword included must be tied to proof.

Acceptable EM proof includes:

#### Leadership Proof

* team size managed
* number of direct reports
* multi-team scope
* hiring / onboarding / mentoring evidence
* cross-functional ownership
* delivery cadence or predictability improvement

#### Technical Proof

* architecture modernization
* reliability improvement
* latency / throughput gains
* cost reduction
* platform scale
* incident reduction
* deployment stability
* engineering standards introduced

#### Business Proof

* revenue-sensitive systems
* customer-impact scale
* platform used by X teams
* budget / cost ownership
* faster time-to-market
* productivity improvement
* operational risk reduction

If no proof exists:

* downgrade the keyword
* move it to another section
* or omit it

---

### 7) Summary Structure

### Sentence 1 — Identity + Scope

State:

* exact or near-exact target title
* seniority
* leadership scope
* technical environment/domain

Good shape:

**Engineering Manager with X+ years leading teams building cloud platforms, distributed systems, or customer-facing products in [domain].**

This sentence must establish that the person is a **leader in a technical environment**, not just a former engineer.

### Sentence 2 — Technical Leadership + Delivery

State:

* how the candidate leads technically
* how they influence execution
* where they retain hands-on credibility, if true

Examples of acceptable themes:

* architecture direction
* modernization programs
* delivery across multiple squads
* engineering standards
* observability / reliability strategy
* incident leadership
* coaching engineers while improving execution quality
* selective hands-on prototyping or deep-dive debugging, if defensible

This sentence should connect **technical authority** with **management effectiveness**.

### Final Sentence or Final Clause — Hard Proof Ending

The summary must **end with concrete proof**.

Required proof types:

* team size
* org/program scope
* platform/system scale
* performance/reliability gain
* cost savings
* hiring / retention evidence
* business or customer impact

Examples:

* “...leading teams of 8–20 engineers across platform and product delivery.”
* “...improving release predictability while reducing incident load by 30%.”
* “...supporting high-traffic systems serving millions of users.”
* “...driving modernization that cut cloud spend by £500K annually.”

Do **not** end on vague differentiators like:

* passionate leader
* strong communicator
* results-driven manager
* thrives in ambiguity

End on evidence.

---

### 8) Tone Rules

The tone must be:

* authoritative
* concise
* executive but not inflated
* technically credible
* human-readable
* specific

Avoid:

* motivational language
* generic leadership clichés
* buzzword stacks
* consultant-speak
* empty adjectives

Preferred language patterns:

* led
* scaled
* built and coached
* delivered
* modernized
* improved
* standardized
* directed
* partnered
* raised
* reduced
* accelerated

Use “built” carefully. For EM roles, prefer verbs that imply **leadership plus execution**, not solo implementation.

---

### 9) Anti-IC Language Rules

Do not let the summary read like a senior IC unless the target role is explicitly player-coach.

Avoid opening with:

* “Senior engineer specializing in...”
* “Hands-on developer with...”
* “Built APIs/microservices using...”
* “Expert in [tool list]...”

Unless the candidate is explicitly applying to a hands-on EM role, do not center the paragraph on coding.

Better patterns:

* “Engineering Manager leading...”
* “Technical people leader delivering...”
* “Engineering leader with a background in...”
* “Backend / platform Engineering Manager combining team leadership with architecture depth...”

Hands-on credibility should appear as:

* architecture leadership
* engineering standards
* system design judgment
* incident response leadership
* modernization ownership
* occasional prototyping / debugging / technical deep dives, if true

---

### 10) ATS Keyword Guidance

Use exact JD phrasing where supported.

For EM roles, prioritize a natural mix of:

#### Leadership Terms

* Engineering Manager
* people leadership
* coaching
* mentoring
* hiring
* stakeholder management
* roadmap delivery
* cross-functional
* Agile / Scrum / Kanban / SAFe
* team scaling
* delivery excellence

#### Technical Terms

* architecture
* distributed systems
* microservices
* cloud
* AWS / Azure / GCP
* APIs
* platform engineering
* reliability
* observability
* CI/CD
* modernization
* performance
* security

#### Outcome Terms

* scale
* uptime
* incident reduction
* delivery predictability
* cloud cost optimization
* productivity improvement
* revenue impact
* customer impact

Rules:

* include only the most relevant subset
* do not dump tools
* do not include a platform just because the candidate touched it once
* technical keywords must support the EM story, not replace it

---

### 11) Template Frameworks

#### Template A — Strict ATS Minimal

[Target title] with [X+ years] leading engineering teams delivering [domain/platform] solutions using [1–2 technical themes]. Combines [leadership signal] with [technical authority signal] to improve [delivery/reliability/scale outcome]. Ends with a concrete proof point such as team size, platform scale, or measurable improvement.

#### Template B — Balanced Technical Leadership

[Target title] with [X+ years] leading teams in [domain], with hands-on credibility in [architecture/platform/stack]. Drives delivery through [leadership mechanism] while setting direction on [technical themes]. Proven by [team/program scope + metric/outcome].

#### Template C — Impact-Forward EM

Engineering leader experienced in scaling teams and delivering [platform/product/domain] initiatives in [technical environment]. Known for combining [technical authority] with [execution/people leadership] to achieve [business/operational result]. Final clause must contain hard evidence.

---

### 12) Quality Gate

The summary must pass all checks:

* opens with an EM-appropriate identity
* stays within 2–3 sentences
* includes both leadership and technical signal
* includes only provable keywords
* sounds like a technical leader, not a stack inventory
* uses ATS-friendly, standard terminology
* avoids fluff and keyword stuffing
* remains defensible in interview
* closes with measurable proof
* feels tailored to the target role, not generic

---

### 13) Common Failure Modes

Avoid:

* sounding like a senior IC with mentoring on the side
* sounding like a generic people manager with no technical depth
* listing too many tools
* vague phrases like “worked on,” “helped with,” “involved in”
* ending with non-evidence-based differentiators
* claiming leadership scope without people evidence
* claiming hands-on depth without recent proof
* writing a summary that could fit any engineering manager

---

### 14) Output Protocol

When the user asks for an EM summary:

1. Extract leadership, technical, and business signals from the JD
2. Match only provable signals to evidence
3. Choose the correct balance:

   * classic people-led EM
   * technical EM
   * player-coach EM
4. Draft 3 options:

   * Strict ATS minimal
   * Balanced technical leadership
   * Impact-forward EM
5. Ensure every version ends with concrete proof
6. If evidence is thin, simplify the claim rather than inflate it
